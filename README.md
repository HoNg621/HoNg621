I am a nucleic acid chemical biologist focused on the dynamics and functions of RNA structures, as well as their small-molecule targeting.

I completed undergraduate training at Jilin University and rotated through several labs at ShanghaiTech, exploring diverse topics including:

- Hepatocyte transdifferentiation (Huang Lab)
- Optimization of GPCR protein purification conditions (Raymond Lab)
- Impact of the HIV Rev protein on the host tRNA pool (Bai Lab)
- Cytoophidia formation under nutrient deprivation (Liu Lab)
- Generation of mosaic knockout mouse models (Chi Lab)

During my Ph.D. in the Bai Lab, I studied mRNA translation delays during meiosis from an RNA structure perspective by profiling the meiotic structurome in yeast. Although **no** significant correlation was found between structural changes and translation efficiency changes, I noticed simpler structures in meiotic mRNAs. This observation led me to uncover an oscillatory translation preference based on RNA structures during meiosis, driven by oscillatory expression of RNA helicases. These findings suggest that cells can reallocate translational resources across transcripts with different structural complexities by modulating their overall unwinding power. When DNA transcription is limited during cellular divisions, reduced unwinding power amplifies the impact of RNA structure on translation, making post-transcriptional regulation dominate gene expression.

I also identified a subset of mRNAs showing unique structural change patterns—a widespread decrease in chemical reactivity—which hinted at double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) formation. I validated the formation of dsRNA aggregates before the first meiotic division and traced their transport to vacuoles for degradation. Furthermore, classical meiotic transcription factors—Ndt80 and Ume6—were identified as being responsible for stage-specific antisense RNA biogenesis. This work shows that dsRNAs can be broadly present in eukaryotes and highlights the dual role of transcription factors in reshaping the transcriptome.

Tragically, 29 days after my graduation ceremony, I received the devastating news that Yun had passed away—without ever having revealed her illness to us. That profound loss made me realize how far medicine still has to go and how long the road ahead is for us researchers. Given that the majority of proteins remain undruggable, I commit to the field of small-molecule RNA targeting, carrying forward Yun's legacy—her knowledge and spirit. My ultimate hope is to contribute alternative tools to combat cancer.

At the end of 2023, I joined the Weeks Lab at UNC Chapel Hill, where I am now developing chemical probes to discover complex, targetable tertiary structures in the human transcriptome. This work is currently ongoing, and what's past is prologue.
