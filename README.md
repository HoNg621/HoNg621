I am a nucleic acid chemical biologist focused on the dynamics and functions of RNA structure, as well as its small-molecule targeting.

I received undergraduate training at Jilin University and rotated through several labs at ShanghaiTech, exploring diverse topics including:

- Hepatocyte transdifferentiation ([Huang Lab](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=WYOg2hkAAAAJ&hl=en))
- GPCR protein purification ([Stevens Lab](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=xP4TuTwAAAAJ&hl=en))
- Impact of the HIV Rev protein on the host tRNA pool ([Bai Lab](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Yun-Bai-6))
- Cytoophidia formation under nutrient deprivation ([Liu Lab](https://scholar.google.com/citations?user=CevGXO0AAAAJ&hl=en))
- Generation of mosaic knockout mouse models ([Chi Lab](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Tian-Chi))

During my Ph.D., I worked with Dr. Yun Bai to study mRNA translation delays during meiosis from an RNA structure perspective by profiling the meiotic structurome in yeast. Although **no correlation** was found between changes in RNA structure and translation efficiency (*that's life*), I noticed that meiotic mRNAs adopt relatively simpler structures. This observation led me to uncover a high-low-high oscillatory translational preference for RNA structure during meiosis, driven by the same oscillatory expression of RNA helicases. This work suggests that cells can tweak overall unwinding power (RNA helicases) to control how mRNAs with different structural complexities get translated—a post-transcriptional regulatory layer whose role becomes crucial and dominant during cell divisions, when DNA becomes compacted and transcription from DNA is limited. (See our paper: [RNA structure coordinates translation across the meiotic program](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.01.15.699792v2))

I also identified a subset of mRNAs showing unique structural change patterns—a widespread decrease in chemical reactivity—which hinted at double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) formation. I validated the formation of dsRNA aggregates before the first meiotic division and traced their transport to vacuoles for degradation. Furthermore, classical meiotic transcription factors—Ndt80p and Ume6p—were identified as activators of stage-specific antisense RNAs. This work shows that dsRNAs can be broadly induced in eukaryotic physiology, and highlights the dual role of transcription factors in reshaping the transcriptome. (See our paper: [Programmed double-stranded RNA formation enables meiotic stage transitions](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.01.20.700650v1))

Tragically, one month after my Ph.D. ceremony (July 2023), the devastating news came that Yun had passed away, without ever having revealed her illness to us. That profound loss made me realize how long the journey ahead will be for us researchers. Given that the majority of proteins remain undruggable, I commit to the field of small-molecule RNA targeting, carrying forward Yun's legacy—her knowledge and spirit. My ultimate hope is to contribute alternative RNA-structure-based tools to combat cancer.

At the end of 2023, I joined the Weeks Lab at UNC Chapel Hill, where I am now developing chemical probes to discover complex, targetable tertiary structures in the human transcriptome. This work is currently ongoing, with more findings to share soon.

As Yun once wrote in her celebration message: *what's past is prologue*.
